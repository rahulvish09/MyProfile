01 Document Hierarchy

QMS Document Structure

A world-class QMS relies on a well-defined 4-level documentation pyramid — from strategic policy to daily records.

Level 1 — Top
IMS Manual · IMS Policy · Quality Manual Strategic direction & management commitment
Level 2 — System
Management System Procedures (MSP) Cross-functional "how" procedures
Level 3 — Ops
Department Manuals · SOPs · Drawings · Emergency Plans Operational procedures & work instructions
Level 4 — Records
Formats · Logbooks · Compliance Registers Evidence — "If it's not recorded, it didn't happen"
L1

Level 1 — Top Level Documents

IMS Manual · IMS Policy · Quality Manual

▼

Purpose

Defines the organization's quality vision, mission, and strategic intent. Establishes the foundation and communicates top management commitment.

Who Uses It

  • Top Management / Directors
  • Management Representative (MR)
  • External auditors & certification bodies

Examples

  • IMS Manual: Scope, exclusions, process interactions
  • IMS Policy: Signed quality, HSE & energy commitments
  • Quality Manual: QMS structure per ISO 9001 / API Q1

Audit Importance

The first document reviewed by any auditor. Demonstrates management commitment, policy alignment, and scope definition.

L2

Level 2 — Management System Procedures

Cross-functional system procedures

▼

Purpose

Define how each standard requirement is implemented across the organization. Bridge between policy and operations.

Examples

  • MSP-01: Document Control
  • MSP-02: Non-Conforming Product
  • MSP-03: Internal Audit
  • MSP-04: Management Review
  • MSP-05: CAPA
  • MSP-06: Purchasing
L3

Level 3 — Operational Documents

SOPs · Drawings · Emergency Plans

▼

Purpose

Step-by-step work-level instructions for operators and engineers. This is where "the work actually happens."

Examples

  • SOP: Hydrostatic Testing of Steel Pipes
  • SOP: Welding Procedure Qualification
  • Engineering drawings, P&IDs
  • Fire emergency & evacuation plans
L4

Level 4 — Records & Formats

Formats · Logbooks · Compliance Registers

▼

Purpose

Provides objective evidence that activities were performed as planned. "If it's not recorded, it didn't happen."

Audit Focus

70–80% of audit time is spent reviewing records — checking legibility, traceability, retention, and controlled amendments.

Hazard Identification & Risk Assessment

HIRA
▼

What is HIRA?

Systematic process of identifying workplace hazards, assessing risks, and determining control measures.

Steps

  1. Identify Activities: List work activities (welding, grinding, crane ops).
  2. Identify Hazards: Physical, chemical, biological, ergonomic.
  3. Identify Who's at Risk: Workers, visitors, contractors.
  4. Assess Risk: Severity × Likelihood matrix → Low/Medium/High/Critical.
  5. Determine Controls: Elimination → Substitution → Engineering → Admin → PPE.
  6. Document & Review: Record in HIRA register, review periodically.

Example: Welding in Pipe Fabrication

ActivityHazardRiskControl
Arc WeldingUV radiation, fumes, burnsHighScreens, extractors, helmet, gloves
GrindingFlying particles, noiseMediumGoggles, ear plugs, guard
Crane LiftingFalling load, crushCriticalCertified crane, signalman, barricading

Identifying elements of activities that interact with the environment and assessing their impact significance. Core requirement of ISO 14001.

Safety Factor (FoS)

Factor of Safety
▼

Ratio of maximum strength to actual load — provides margin for uncertainties in materials, loading, and manufacturing.

FoS = Ultimate Strength / Working Stress
FoS = Maximum Load Capacity / Actual Applied Load
ApplicationTypical FoSReason
Pressure Vessels3.0–4.0Catastrophic failure risk
Cranes / Lifting4.0–6.0Dynamic loads, human safety
Pipes (Oil & Gas)2.0–3.5High pressure, corrosion
Aircraft1.2–1.5Weight-critical, extensively tested
🔄

Management of Change

MOC
▼

Systematic process to evaluate, approve, and implement changes while managing associated risks.

MOC Process

  1. Initiate: Document proposed change with justification.
  2. Risk Assessment: Evaluate quality, safety, environment impact.
  3. Review & Approve: Cross-functional team review.
  4. Implement: Controlled rollout, update procedures, train.
  5. Verify & Close: Confirm results without adverse effects.
⚠️
Uncontrolled changes are a top root cause of quality failures and safety incidents. MOC is mandatory in API Q1 and ISO 45001.
🔍

Internal Audit

First-Party
▼

Systematic, independent examination to verify QMS conformance and effectiveness. Conducted at least annually covering all departments and clauses.

Process

  1. Annual audit plan covering all processes
  2. Select independent, qualified auditors
  3. Review procedures, records, and previous findings
  4. Opening meeting → Execution → Evidence collection
  5. Classify findings: Major NC / Minor NC / Observation
  6. Closing meeting → CAPA follow-up → Management Review
🏛️

External Audit

Certification & Surveillance
▼

Certification Audit (Stage 1 & 2)

Stage 1: Documentation review. Stage 2: On-site audit. Certificate valid for 3 years.

Surveillance Audit

Annual check on continued compliance. Covers subset of requirements, focuses on changes and CAPAs.

Customer Audit (Second-Party)

Conducted by customers (ONGC, Saudi Aramco, Shell) to evaluate supplier capability.

04 Industry Application

Practical Industry Examples

How QMS operates daily in a steel pipe manufacturing & fabrication plant.

⚙️
📦

Incoming Material Inspection

When raw materials arrive:

  • MTC verified against purchase order
  • Chemical & mechanical properties cross-checked
  • Material tagged with unique heat number
  • Non-conforming material → NCR raised
⚙️

In-Process Quality Control

During manufacturing:

  • Welding parameters per WPS
  • NDE — UT, RT, MPI, DPI
  • Heat treatment cycle recorded
  • ITP hold/witness points enforced
✅

Final Inspection & Hydro Test

Before dispatch:

  • Hydrostatic test at 1.5× design pressure
  • Final dimensional verification
  • Test certificates with full traceability
  • Customer TPI witness if required
🦺

Daily Safety & HSE

  • Toolbox talks before every shift
  • Permit-to-Work for hot/confined/height work
  • PPE compliance & near-miss reporting
  • Emergency mock drills quarterly
📁

Document Control

  • Master document list maintained
  • Controlled copies with stamps issued
  • Revision history tracked
  • Obsolete copies recalled
👷

Role of Engineers

  • QA: Plans inspections, audits, CAPA
  • QC: Tests, records, NCR identification
  • Production: Follows SOPs, reports deviations
  • Safety: HIRA, training, incident investigation
05 Critical Awareness

What Happens If QMS Is NOT Implemented?

The consequences are severe, far-reaching, and can be catastrophic.

🚨
🚨
Real-World: Bhopal Gas Tragedy, Deepwater Horizon, Piper Alpha — all linked to management system failures. Thousands of deaths, billions in losses.
💀

Safety Risks

Uncontrolled hazards → injuries & fatalities. No safety culture means no accident prevention.

⚖️

Legal Issues

Violations, fines, plant shutdown, criminal prosecution of directors.

🚫

Customer Rejection

Delisted from vendor lists. Products rejected without proper certificates & traceability.

💸

Financial Loss

Scrap, rework, warranty claims — failure costs can consume 15-25% of revenue.

🏭

Production Failure

Inconsistent output, machine breakdowns, missed deliveries, unpredictable quality.

📉

Reputation Damage

One major incident = permanent reputation loss. Lost contracts, talent, and market position.
